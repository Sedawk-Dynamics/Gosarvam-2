Why Assam's second flush is the world's most coveted tea

By the Gosarvam editorial desk · Published 2025 Share →

There are tea drinkers who can taste a season in a single sip. They will hold the cup, breathe slowly, and tell you — yes, this is second flush, picked between May and June, when the leaf was full but not yet tired.

Assam, the lush region of north-eastern India cradled between the Brahmaputra and the foothills of the Himalayas, is the most prolific tea region on earth — and the second flush, plucked over a brief six-week window every summer, is its crowning expression. The leaves are darker, the cup is brighter, and the flavour carries a peculiar maltiness that no other tea in the world can imitate.

"The first flush is poetry. The second flush is autobiography." — A common saying among Assam tea-masters.

The micro-climate that does the work

By late May, the monsoon has begun touching the Brahmaputra basin. Daytime temperatures hover around 28–32°C; nights are humid, warm, and rich with rainfall. The tea bush, which has spent the cool months of April recovering from the first flush, now bursts into a thicker, glossier, more pigment-dense leaf.

This is also when the tea mosquito bug — a tiny, fussy insect — descends on the gardens. Their bite stresses the plant, and the plant responds with a defensive compound called 2,6-dimethyl-2,6-octadien-8-ol. To you, the drinker, that translates into a complex, faintly woody, faintly fruity character that has come to define second flush Assam.

180 years of refinement

Commercial tea cultivation in Assam began in 1837, when Robert Bruce confirmed that the wild tea plants the Singpho tribe had been brewing for generations were genuine Camellia sinensis assamica. Within two decades, the region became the largest tea-producing zone in the British Empire.

What survived is not the colonial estate model — that is largely gone — but the craft. The plucking standards. The withering troughs. The orthodox rolling. The fired finish.

  • Two leaves and a bud — picked by hand, twice daily, in the early morning.
  • Withering — leaves are spread on troughs and air-cooled for 12–18 hours.
  • Orthodox rolling — gentle, slow, preserves leaf integrity.
  • Oxidation — under controlled humidity, until the perfect copper.
  • Firing — final hot-air drying to lock in volatile aromatics.

What a buyer should look for

If you are sourcing second flush Assam, three markers separate the great from the ordinary: grade, elevation, and single-estate traceability. A SFTGFOP1 grade (Super Fine Tippy Golden Flowery Orange Pekoe, Grade 1) tells you the leaf is intact, tippy, well-sorted, and orthodox-processed. Elevation between 1500–2500 ft yields the best balance of body and aroma. And insisting on single-estate sourcing protects against blending shortcuts that hide character behind volume.
